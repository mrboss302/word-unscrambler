Which word tool should you use?

All five tools search the same dictionary, but each one is shaped for a different kind of problem. Picking the right one saves you time:

You have a rack of letters and want every word

Reach for the Word Unscrambler. Type in your tiles — say AEINRST — and it returns every word you can build from them, grouped from longest to shortest with a Scrabble score on each. This is the tool for Scrabble and Words With Friends racks, or for cracking a Jumble. If you also need to force a prefix, a suffix, or a specific length, the Word Solver adds those filters on top of the same letter search.

You are playing a five-letter guessing game

Use the Wordle Helper. It only searches five-letter words and lets you enter the green, yellow, and gray clues from your previous guesses, then shows the words that still fit. If you just want to browse or filter five-letter words without clues — for example, every word that starts with ST and ends in E — the 5 Letter Words finder is faster.

You want to rearrange a name or phrase

The Anagram Generator ignores spaces and punctuation, so you can drop in a full name or short phrase and see the words hidden inside it. It finds both full anagrams (every letter used) and sub-anagrams (some letters used), which is handy for puzzles, team names, and wordplay.

A quick glossary of word-game terms

Word games come with their own vocabulary. Here are the terms you will run into most often, in plain English:

Anagram

A word or phrase made by rearranging all the letters of another, using each letter exactly once. Listen and silent are anagrams of each other.

Sub-anagram

A shorter word made from some of the available letters rather than all of them. From the letters in spare you can make the sub-anagrams pear, reap, and ear.

Rack

The set of letter tiles a player holds in Scrabble or Words With Friends — usually seven. Your rack is what you type into the unscrambler.

Bingo (or bonus)

Playing all seven tiles in a single Scrabble turn, which earns a 50-point bonus. Seven- and eight-letter results are worth scanning for this reason.

Hook

A letter added to the front or back of a word already on the board to make a new word — turning are into hare or ares. The starts-with and ends-with filters help you find hooks.

Two-letter words

Short words like qi, za, and ax that are the backbone of tight board play. They let you connect to existing words in cramped spaces.

Green, yellow, and gray

Wordle feedback colors: green means the right letter in the right spot, yellow means the letter is in the word but elsewhere, and gray means the letter is not in the word at all.

Opener

The first word you guess in Wordle. A strong opener uses common letters and several different vowels to reveal as much as possible on turn one.

Tips that work across every word game

Learn the short words first

The two- and three-letter words do the heavy lifting in board games. They let you play parallel to existing words and squeeze onto bonus squares. Memorizing a core set of valid short words improves your scores more than chasing rare long words.

Balance your rack

A rack that is all vowels or all consonants is hard to play. When you have a choice, keep a mix so your next turn has options. If you are stuck with duplicate awkward tiles, it is often worth swapping rather than forcing a weak play.

Spend high-value tiles wisely

Q, Z, J, and X score big but can clog a rack. Look for the short words that use them — and remember that in many games Q can be played without a U. The score badge next to each result helps you cash these tiles in.

In Wordle, gather information early

Your first one or two guesses are most valuable as information, not as attempts to win. Test a broad spread of common letters, then let the Wordle Helper narrow the field once you have a few greens and yellows.
