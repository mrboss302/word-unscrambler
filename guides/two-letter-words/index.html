Two-Letter Words Worth Knowing

The smallest words do the biggest work. Knowing a solid set of two-letter words is the single fastest way to raise your scores in Scrabble and Words With Friends — here is why, and which ones to learn first.

Why two-letter words win games

Two-letter words are the connective tissue of a board. They let you play alongside an existing word — a parallel play — so a single tile can form two or three short words at once and rack up points from a cramped space. They are also how you reach a bonus square that a longer word cannot fit, and how you offload an awkward tile without surrendering your turn.

Crucially, a parallel play scores every new word it creates. Slide a tile next to three existing letters so it forms three legal two-letter words, and you score all three. That is why strong players memorize short words before they chase long ones.

The short words worth memorizing

Many of the most useful two-letter plays are ordinary words you already know: at, in, on, to, up, it, is, an, as, be, by, do, go, he, hi, if, me, my, no, of, or, so, we. The ones that surprise newcomers are the less obvious entries that are still widely accepted in word games, such as:

  • aa, ae, ai, oe, oi — handy vowel-heavy plays that absorb spare vowels.
  • qi and za — the two highest-impact plays of all, because they place a Q (with no U) or a Z for big points.
  • xu, xi, ax, ox — short ways to spend an X.
  • jo — a tidy spot for a J.
  • em, en, es, et, ut, mm, od, op, ka, ki — filler plays that keep a turn alive.

You do not need to memorize all of them at once. Start with the high-value ones — qi, za, and the X words — because they turn your most troublesome tiles into points.

How to actually play them

The trick is to look for places where one new tile lines up against existing letters. Suppose the board has the word cat and you place a tile directly above the a to make a longer word; if that tile also forms a legal two-letter word with the c or the t beside it, you score both. Scanning for these overlaps is a skill that comes quickly once you know which short words are legal.

When you are staring at an awkward rack, drop your tiles into the Word Unscrambler and look at the two-letter group. To search for short words that contain a specific tile — every two-letter word with a Q, say — the Word Solver lets you set the length to two and require that letter.

A word on official dictionaries

Different games use different official word lists, and the set of valid two-letter words is exactly where they disagree most. A two-letter play that is legal in one game may be rejected in another. Word Puzzle Solver is not affiliated with any game publisher and offers general puzzle help, not official rulings — so when a short word really matters, check it against the official dictionary of the game you are playing.
