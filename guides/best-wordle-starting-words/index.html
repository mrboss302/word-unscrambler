The Best Wordle Starting Words (and Why They Work)

A good opening guess is not magic — it is arithmetic. The best starters test the letters that appear most often, so you walk into your second guess with as much information as possible. Here is how to pick one.

What makes an opener good

Three things separate a strong opener from a weak one. First, it should use five different letters — a repeated letter wastes one of your five test slots. Second, it should favor common letters, because common letters are more likely to appear in the answer. Third, it should include two or three vowels, since every answer has at least one and pinning the vowels down early collapses the field fast.

In five-letter English words, the letters you meet most often are roughly E, A, R, O, T, L, I, S, N, and C. Vowels cluster near the top because almost every word needs them. A great opener is simply a real word that packs as many of these as possible into five distinct slots.

Solid single-word openers

Any of these are strong first guesses. They share the common-letter, two-or-three-vowel recipe and use no repeats:

  • slate — S, L, A, T, E: five very common letters with two of the most frequent vowels.
  • crane — covers C, R, A, N, E, mixing top consonants with two vowels.
  • trace — same useful letters as crane in a different shape.
  • stare — S, T, A, R, E: four of the ten most common letters plus an early A and E.
  • adieu — four vowels (A, I, E, U) for players who like to lock vowels first.
  • raise — balances R, S, and N-adjacent frequency with two vowels.

There is no single "correct" answer — what matters is that you pick a word built from common letters and use it consistently so you learn how it behaves.

Two-word openers: testing ten letters

Many strong players fix two opening words that, together, test ten different common letters before they start deducing. The idea is to spend the first two guesses gathering information, then solve with the remaining attempts. Pairs that cover ten distinct common letters include:

  • trace then lions — together they test T, R, A, C, E, L, I, O, N, S.
  • slate then corny — covers S, L, A, T, E, C, O, R, N, Y.
  • roast then lined — covers R, O, A, S, T, L, I, N, E, D.

The trade-off is that you commit two guesses to discovery, so two-word openers suit players chasing consistency more than a lucky two-guess win.

After the opener: turn clues into a short list

Once your first guess comes back colored, stop guessing by feel and start narrowing. Enter what you know into the Wordle Helper: greens go in the pattern field at their exact spots, letters that came up yellow go in the include field (with the positions they cannot occupy), and grays go in the exclude field. The helper returns the five-letter words that still satisfy every clue, so you can choose your next guess deliberately.

If several candidates remain, prefer a guess that tests new letters over a hopeful answer — eliminating possibilities is usually worth more than a long-shot win. And if you just want to browse five-letter words by shape, the 5 Letter Words finder lets you filter by starting letter, ending letter, and the letters in between.
